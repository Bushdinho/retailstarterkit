🚢
Sea Freight Consolidation Rule

Sea freight is charged by box volume — not by weight. The consolidation rule for sea is about minimum spend, not minimum kg. Only ship when your total goods value is at least $1,500–$2,500 USD (or £1,200–£2,000 GBP). Below this threshold, the agent fee and handling costs consume too high a share of your goods value to leave a healthy margin. Accumulate stock across multiple buying trips and ship when you reach your value minimum — not before.

✈
Air Freight Consolidation Rule

Air freight is charged per kg. As a general guide, aim for at least 10–15 kg per air shipment to keep your per-item cost reasonable. Below this, the minimum handling charges from agents mean you pay disproportionately for a small package. Only use air freight for high-value, fast-selling items where the speed premium is justified — for example, restocking a bestseller for a pre-paid pre-order.

The Retail Arbitrage Model — Why It Works in Ghana
"Before you can scale a business, you must understand exactly why it makes money — and what threatens it."

Retail arbitrage is one of the oldest and most reliable business models in the world. The concept is simple: buy goods where they are cheap, sell them where they are expensive. The profit is the difference between the two prices, minus your costs to move the goods from one market to the other.

In Ghana's context, this model is particularly powerful because of a combination of factors that create a wide and consistent price gap between US/UK retail prices and what Ghana consumers are willing to pay for the same items.

Why the Gap Exists — and Why It Will Stay

Five structural factors create and sustain the price gap you profit from. Each factor is independent — meaning even if one weakens, the other four continue holding the gap open. This is why the model has worked for 12+ years and shows no sign of closing.

1
Factor 1
US/UK Brands Run Seasonal Clearance Cycles
Every season — January, June/July, November — major brands must clear old stock to make room for new arrivals. They discount 50–80% to do it fast. These windows are predictable, annual, and reliable. You can plan your buying capital around them months in advance. This is the engine of the entire business model.
2
Factor 2
Ghana Has No Local Supply of Premium Branded Goods
Zara, Coach, Michael Kors, Aldo, and similar brands do not have stores in Ghana and cannot be purchased locally at any price. If a Ghanaian customer wants a genuine Coach bag or a pair of original Aldo shoes, they must go through an importer — or go without. You are not competing with a local alternative. You are the only option.
3
Factor 3
Ghana's Growing Middle Class Has Strong Appetite for Premium Brands
Aspirational consumption in Ghana is growing — not declining. A rising professional class wants authentic branded goods as a statement of success. They are not looking for the cheapest option; they are looking for the real thing at a price they can justify. This means your customers have genuine willingness to pay premium prices for genuine products.
4
Factor 4
The Currency Differential Amplifies the Price Gap
A Coach bag that retails at $295 in the US is purchased at the outlet for $45–$65 at clearance. At a rate of GH₵ 15/USD, that item costs you approximately GH₵ 675–975 landed. You price it at GH₵ 2,800–3,200. The currency gap between the dollar and the cedi means the arbitrage margin is far wider than it would be in a single-currency market. And because the cedi tends to depreciate over time, this gap generally widens — not narrows.
5
Factor 5
High Barriers to Entry Keep Competition Manageable
Setting up this supply chain requires knowing how to find and vet a buyer in the US or UK, how to send money internationally without losing it to bank rates, how to store goods pre-shipment, how to navigate a freight agent, and how to clear customs efficiently. Most people do not know how to do any of these things — and most who try give up before completing the system. This knowledge is your moat. The more you master it, the harder your business becomes to copy.
📊
Real Numbers From Our Business

We started this business 12 years ago with a small buying budget. Today we turn over GH₵ 2.4 million per year — from the same model. The fundamentals have not changed: buy at maximum discount in US/UK, ship efficiently, sell in Ghana at 2.5–3× landed cost. What has changed is our volume, our network, and our systems.

The 3 Threats to Your Model — Know Them Early

  • Currency risk: When the cedi drops, your margin shrinks unless you've priced with a buffer. This is covered in Tool 4 of this kit.
  • Supply inconsistency: Relying on one buyer or one source creates fragility. Always be building your second and third option.
  • Dead stock: Buying what you think will sell vs what you know will sell. Always buy based on what has sold before, not on personal taste.

Numbers · Day 2 of 5
The Profit Numbers Every Ghana Importer Must Know
"If you do not know these 6 numbers for your business right now, you are flying blind — and blind businesses fail."

Most Ghana clothing retailers are busy. They buy, they sell, they hustle — but very few know their actual numbers. They have a rough sense that they're making money, but they can't tell you their margin per item, their real cost per shipment, or what the cedi falling 10% does to their profit.

This is the most common cause of businesses that are always busy but never growing. Profit lives in your numbers, not in your hustle.

The 6 Numbers You Must Know

Track these 6 numbers for your business every month. If you do not know them right now, stop and calculate them before reading further. They are not optional — they are the steering wheel of your business.

1
Landed Cost Per Item (GH₵)
The total cost of one item arriving at your Ghana warehouse — goods purchase price + buyer fee + your share of shipping + duties + clearance. Every single cost included.
Why it matters: Without this number, you cannot know if you are actually profitable on each sale. Many importers who think they're making money are not, because they only calculate the shop price — not the true landed cost.
2
Your Markup Multiple
Formula: Selling Price ÷ Buffered Landed Cost
Healthy target: 2.2× minimum. 2.5× standard. 3.0× premium items. Below 2.2× and you cannot absorb exchange rate movement, overheads, and still profit.
Why it matters: This tells you at a glance whether a buying decision is sound before you spend the money.
3
Gross Margin %
Formula: (Selling Price − Buffered Landed Cost) ÷ Selling Price × 100
Target: Minimum 55% gross margin after your buffered landed cost. Below 55% means your overhead expenses will likely push you into unprofitability.
Why it matters: The percentage of every sale that is actually yours — before rent, staff, and other overheads are paid.
4
Cost Per Kg Shipped
Formula: Total Shipping Bill ÷ Total Kg of That Shipment
Track this number on every shipment. Over time, a falling cost-per-kg tells you your consolidation strategy is working. A rising number signals you are shipping too small.
Why it matters: Your benchmark for shipping efficiency. Use Tool 1 in this kit to calculate and track it.
5
Days of Inventory Outstanding (DIO)
Formula: Average days from shipment arrival to sell-out
Target: Under 45 days for fast movers. Under 90 days for everything. Any item over 90 days is dead stock — activate your liquidation strategy immediately.
Why it matters: The faster your stock turns, the more buying cycles you run per year — and the more money you make with the same capital.
6
Monthly Net Cash Flow
Formula: Cash In from Sales − Cash Out (buying + shipping + overheads)
This is your business's heartbeat. Positive every month means you are building wealth. Negative means something in the chain — buying, pricing, or overheads — is broken and needs to be fixed immediately.
Why it matters: The only number that tells you definitively whether the business is working. All other numbers serve this one.
The Quick Margin Check — Do This For Every Product
Gross Margin = (Selling Price − Landed Cost) ÷ Selling Price × 100
Target: Minimum 55% gross margin after buffered landed cost
Example: Sell GH₵ 2,400 − Landed GH₵ 1,050 = GH₵ 1,350 profit = 56% margin ✓
⚠️
The Dangerous Assumption

"I sell for 3× what I paid, so I'm making good money." This is only true if you're calculating landed cost — not just what you paid the shop. Many importers forget to include shipping, duties, buyer fees, storage, and their own time. When you add all costs, 3× on the shop price can become 1.8× on true landed cost — not enough to survive a cedi move.

✏️
Today's Action

Pick your best-selling product. Use Tool 2 in this kit to calculate its true landed cost. Then calculate the gross margin on what you currently sell it for. If it's below 55% on the buffered landed cost — you need to either raise your price or source at a better discount.

Day 2 Key Takeaways
1
Landed cost is your true cost — not the shop price. Include every cost that touches your goods.
2
Minimum 2.2× markup on buffered landed cost. Below that, the business doesn't survive exchange rate reality.
3
Know your 6 numbers. Businesses that don't know their numbers are managed by guesswork, not strategy.

Supply · Day 3 of 5
Building a Supply Chain That Never Runs Dry
"Supply inconsistency is the second-biggest profit killer. Here's how to build a chain that keeps your shelves stocked."

Your business can only make money when you have stock to sell. Yet the most common complaint we hear from Ghana retail importers is: "I had a great selling period, then my stock ran out and I had nothing for two months." This is a supply chain design problem — and it's completely solvable.

The 3-Layer Supply Chain

A resilient supply chain has three independent layers. If one layer fails — your buyer gets sick, a website blocks your payment, a sale window is missed — the other two keep your business running. Most small importers only have Layer 1. That means one unavailable person can stop the entire business. Here is what all three layers look like and how to build them:

Layer
1
Primary
Your Primary Buyer in the US or UK
A trusted person — family member, friend, or vetted contact — who physically goes to outlet malls and stores, selects your items, sends you photos before purchase, and holds the goods until your shipper collects them. This person handles 70–80% of your buying volume.
Most importers have this, or are building it. ✓
Layer
2
Online
Online Shopping + Parcel Forwarder
A parcel forwarding address in the US or UK (e.g. MyUS.com or UK Postbox) that receives online orders. You or your buyer shops brand websites and outlet clearance sections online, ships to the forwarding address, and the forwarder holds and consolidates until ready to send to Ghana. This supplements your physical buyer and captures online-only deals.
Few importers have this set up. Takes 15 minutes to open an account.
Layer
3
Backup
A Second Buyer in a Different City or Country
A vetted second buyer — ideally in a different city (e.g. Buyer A in New York, Buyer B in London) or covering a different product speciality (e.g. one for clothing, one for bags and accessories). This layer activates when your primary buyer is unavailable, provides geographic access to different outlet malls, and lets you run parallel buying trips to scale volume.
Almost nobody has this. It is a genuine competitive advantage — and the key to scaling past one-buyer dependency.

The Seasonal Buying Calendar — Your Supply Planning Tool

Use the calendar in Tool 3 to plan your buying 3–6 months in advance. The mistake most importers make is reactive buying — they wait until they run out of stock, then scramble. The system that works is proactive buying — you know the January sale is coming in December, so you plan your budget, brief your buyer, and execute on day one of the sale rather than week two when the best items are gone.

💡
The 3-Month Forward Planning Rule

At any given point, you should have: (1) stock currently in Ghana being sold, (2) a shipment in transit (5–9 weeks away), and (3) a buying plan confirmed for the next buying cycle. If you're only thinking one step ahead, you will always have stock gaps.

Knowing What to Buy — The Data-First Approach

New importers buy what they like. Experienced importers buy what has sold. Before every buying cycle, review your last 3 months of sales data and answer these questions:

  • Which 3 items sold fastest? — Buy more of these (same or similar styles)
  • Which 3 items are still sitting? — Do not restock these until they clear
  • What did customers ask for that you didn't have? — Add these to your list
  • Which size/colour combinations moved fastest? — Skew future orders accordingly
✏️
Today's Action

Write your current supply chain status: Do you have Layer 1, 2, and 3? Identify which layer is missing. Then plan one action to strengthen your weakest layer this week — even if it's just opening a parcel forwarder account (Layer 2 takes 10 minutes).

Day 3 Key Takeaways
1
Build 3 supply layers. One buyer is not a supply chain — it's a dependency.
2
Plan 3 months forward. Always have stock selling, stock in transit, and a next buy planned.
3
Buy from data, not taste. Your customers' behaviour is a better buying guide than your personal preference.

Money · Day 4 of 5
Managing Money — Cash Flow, Currency and Capital
"The cedi has fallen over 50% in the last 5 years. If your business isn't built to handle that, you don't have a business — you have a gamble."

Three money problems kill Ghana clothing importers more than anything else. Not competition. Not bad products. Not a slow economy. It's these three: cash locked in transit, pricing at the wrong exchange rate, and no system to keep capital flowing between buying cycles.

Problem 1 — Cash Locked in Transit

Sea freight from the US takes 8–9 weeks. UK takes 5–7 weeks. That means when you buy in January, your capital is locked until March or April. Meanwhile, you have bills to pay, staff to run, and potentially another buying window (Valentine's, outlet sales) that you can't capitalise on.

The solution is the pre-order model. The moment your goods leave the US or UK, announce to your customer list that stock is on its way. Take deposits of 30–50%. This puts cash in your hands weeks before the goods arrive, and funds your next buying cycle while the current one is still at sea.

📊
Pre-Order Example

You ship GH₵ 18,000 worth of goods in January. You announce the incoming stock on WhatsApp. 8 customers place pre-orders with 40% deposits. That's GH₵ 7,200 collected in January — while stock is still at sea. You use GH₵ 5,000 of that to fund a small February outlet buy. Both cycles run simultaneously.

Problem 2 — Pricing at Today's Rate

You buy in January at GH₵ 14.50/USD. You price your goods based on GH₵ 14.50. By March when goods arrive, the rate is GH₵ 16.00. Your cost in cedis just increased by 10% — but your prices haven't moved. You've already lost money before selling a single item.

The solution is the 15% buffer rule. Always price as if the rate is 15% higher than today. If the rate doesn't move, you make a better-than-expected margin. If it moves against you, you're protected. Use Tool 4 in this kit to apply this every time.

Problem 3 — No Capital Recycling System

The businesses that scale fastest are those where cash turns the fastest. You sell → immediately buy again → sell again. The ones that stay small are the ones where cash sits idle — in a bank account "waiting," in slow-moving stock, or in personal spending between buying cycles.

The Capital Velocity Target
Target: 4 full buying cycles per year
Cycle time = buying + transit (avg 8 weeks) + selling (avg 4–6 weeks) = 12–14 weeks
With pre-orders and staggered shipments → push toward 5–6 cycles per year
✏️
Today's Action

Calculate how many buying cycles you completed in the last 12 months. If it's less than 4, identify where the time is being lost — is it the transit gap, slow selling, or delayed decision-making on the next buy? That gap is your next improvement target.

Day 4 Key Takeaways
1
Pre-orders solve the transit cash gap. Take 30–50% deposits the moment goods leave US/UK.
2
Always price with a 15% exchange rate buffer. Protect your margin before you buy, not after you sell.
3
Capital velocity is profit. 5 cycles at 60% margin beats 3 cycles at 70% margin every time.

The Difference Between a Shop and a System — How to Scale
"You don't scale a business by working harder. You scale it by building systems that work without you."

You've made it to Day 5. Today we talk about the thing that every clothing retailer wants but few build toward deliberately: a business that grows even when you're not watching every detail.

The difference between a GH₵ 200,000/year clothing business and a GH₵ 2,000,000/year clothing business is not talent, luck, or location. It is systems. Specifically, 5 systems that most small retailers never build.

The 5 Systems of a Scalable Clothing Retail Business

Each system below is a distinct, buildable part of your business. Read each one carefully and ask yourself honestly: do I have this system in place, or am I relying on memory, habit, or luck?

1
The Sourcing System
Vetted buyers + calendar-driven buying plan
Without This System
You scramble before every buying trip. You pay too much because you miss the best sale windows. You rely on one contact who is sometimes unavailable. Your stock is inconsistent.
With This System
You buy at maximum discount every cycle because you know the sale calendar. You have two vetted buyers — a primary and a backup. Your inventory is consistent and predictable.
2
The Pricing System
Landed cost calculator + cedi buffer + markup tiers by category
Without This System
You guess your margins. When the cedi drops, your profit disappears. You price clothing, bags, and accessories all the same way even though they have very different margin profiles.
With This System
Every item is priced before you buy it. Your cedi buffer protects margins even if the rate moves 20% against you. Premium items priced at 3.0× generate 40% more revenue on the same buying budget.
3
The Cash Flow System
Pre-orders + staggered shipments + capital recycling plan
Without This System
Your capital is dead for 8–9 weeks every cycle while goods are at sea. You miss buying opportunities. You run 2–3 cycles a year when you could run 5. You are always behind.
With This System
Pre-order deposits fund your next buying cycle while goods are still at sea. You run two cycles in parallel. Capital is always moving. 5+ buying cycles per year on the same starting budget.
4
The Sales System
WhatsApp broadcast + content + loyalty + repeat buyers
Without This System
Customers find you randomly. Revenue is feast-or-famine — strong when stock arrives, then dead. You have no way to reach your past customers when new stock comes in. Dead stock piles up.
With This System
A WhatsApp broadcast of 300+ past buyers generates immediate sales on every arrival. Pre-order campaigns sell 30–40% of your stock before it lands. Revenue is predictable month to month.
5
The Operations System
SOPs + staff training + inventory management + delivery
Without This System
You are the business. When you travel, get sick, or are busy, the business stops. Staff make errors because nothing is written down. Deliveries are unreliable and damage your reputation.
With This System
The business operates and generates sales without you needing to be present for every transaction. Trained staff follow documented SOPs. Vetted riders deliver reliably. You can open a second location.
